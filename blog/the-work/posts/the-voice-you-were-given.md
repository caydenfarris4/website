# The Voice You Were Given

*The Work · Vol. 6*

The first three chapters of my book were unreadable.

I know that now. I did not know it when I wrote them. I thought they were good. I thought they were publishable. I had spent weeks on them, late nights at my desk, trying to get every sentence right.

And then I went back and read them.

I heard it immediately. Not my voice. Someone else's. A blend of every leadership writer I had ever admired, flattened into a version of me that did not exist. The sentences were fine. The structure was fine. The content was technically correct.

But it was not alive.

It was a ghost of other people's voices wearing my name on the cover.

I deleted them. I started over.

Now, this post is not about writing. It is about what I was trying to do, and why so many of us do it without ever realizing we are doing it.

I was trying to be John Maxwell. Or Stephen Covey. Or Viktor Frankl. I had read them for years. I had studied their rhythms, their transitions, their cadence. And when I sat down to write my own book, the voice that came out was a composite of all of them. It was polished. It was correct. It was nothing.

Because there is already a John Maxwell.

There is already a Stephen Covey.

There is already a Jeffrey R. Holland, whose conference addresses I could probably recite from memory, because I have loved his voice since I was a teenager.

God did not need another one of them.

He needed me.

I want you to sit with that, because I do not think most people actually believe it about themselves. We read our mentors. We study our heroes. We watch the ones who have arrived. And somewhere along the way, without ever deciding to do it, we start believing that the way to become useful is to sound like the people who already are.

It is not.

The people who are useful sound like themselves. That is how they got there in the first place. They did not arrive by imitating someone else. They arrived by mining what was already in them, shaping it, sharpening it, and offering it to the world without apology.

And here is the subtle thing that took me too long to understand.

Your mentors do not want you to become them. The real ones, anyway. John Maxwell has spent his life developing other leaders. He does not want an army of Maxwell clones walking around quoting him back to him. He wants an army of leaders who took what he gave them and built their own house with it.

Elder Holland has spent his life teaching people about Jesus Christ. He does not want imitators of his cadence. He wants men and women who will testify of the Savior in their own words, from their own lives, in their own voices.

Mentors give you materials. They do not give you the blueprint.

The blueprint is yours.

Let me say that again.

The blueprint is yours.

Nobody else has the combination of experiences you have. Nobody else has your specific wins and your specific losses. Nobody else has your particular mother, your particular bishop, your particular coach, your particular heartbreak, your particular moment on your knees at two in the morning when you finally understood something you had heard your whole life.

Those are the raw materials the Lord used to build your voice.

And if you suppress them to sound like somebody else, you are not being humble.

You are rejecting the gift.

I want to draw a careful distinction here, because I do not want you to misread this.

Learning from mentors is not imitation. Absorbing principles from people who have gone before you is not copying. I have built my leadership thinking on Maxwell. I have built my scripture study on the Hebrew word studies I learned from scholars and apostles and serious students of the text. I have been shaped by Covey, by Frankl, by Holland, by bishops and coaches and mission presidents and a hundred other voices I will never stop being grateful for.

But shaping is not replacement.

You can learn a leader's principles and deliver them in your own voice. You can absorb a scholar's method and write with your own cadence. You can be mentored by the best and still sound like you. That is what real inheritance looks like. You take what they gave you, you add what only you can add, and you hand the whole thing to the next generation better than you received it.

Imitation is a phase. Every artist goes through it. Every writer goes through it. Every leader goes through it. I went through it for the first three chapters of my book.

The problem is not that you pass through imitation.

The problem is if you stay there.

Because if you stay there, the world never gets you.

And here is what I want you to really hear.

The world does not need more copies of the voices it already has. The world needs the voice you were given. The specific one. The one built from your specific life. The one shaped by the specific hands of God across the specific years you have walked this earth.

Somebody out there is waiting to hear what only you can say.

Not what Maxwell already said. Not what Elder Holland already said. What you have to say, in the language that came out of your own life, through the instrument God spent your entire existence building.

If you do not offer that voice, they do not hear it.

That is the weight of this. It is not just about authenticity for its own sake. It is about the people you were called to reach. The specific souls the Lord had in mind when He shaped your voice. They are waiting. And they are not going to be reached by a copy of somebody else, because somebody else is already reaching the people somebody else was called to reach.

You have a territory. It is yours.

I have a friend who is a gifted speaker. Every time he gets up to speak, he tries to sound like the General Authorities. The cadence. The measured pauses. The turns of phrase. He is kind, he is faithful, and he is a good man. But when he speaks, I feel very little. Because I am not hearing him. I am hearing an echo of voices I already know.

The few times I have heard him speak as himself, not trying, just telling the story of something that happened to him that week, I have been moved to tears.

That voice is the one God gave him.

The other voice is a costume.

I deleted those three chapters. I started over. I wrote the book in my voice, from my job site, with my fingerprints all over every page. It is not the best book ever written. It was not written by the most experienced leader in the world. It was written by a twenty-two-year-old Product Manager who was still building his own house while trying to help others build theirs.

That is the only book I could write.

And I think that is the only book the Lord needed me to write.

Now, I want you to ask yourself something. When you speak, when you write, when you lead, when you teach, whose voice is coming out? Is it yours? Or is it a composite of the people you admire, flattened into a version of you that does not quite exist?

If it is the second one, it is time to stop.

Not stop learning from them. Not stop admiring them. Not stop studying them.

Stop being them.

Write the sentence the way you would say it out loud. Teach the lesson the way you would teach it to a friend over dinner. Lead the team the way the person you actually are would lead them. Testify of the Savior the way only you could testify of Him, built from the specific moments He has carried you through.

The voice you were given is enough.

It was always enough.

The only question is whether you are going to use it.
