I drove out to Fish Springs National Wildlife Refuge alone one weekend.

If you have never been, Fish Springs sits in the west desert of Utah, about as far from anything as you can get and still be in the state. Salt flats in every direction. Mountains on the horizon. A wildlife refuge in the middle of the desert that has no business being there, fed by underground springs that nobody fully understands.

I went out there because I needed to think. Not about anything specific. I just needed the kind of quiet that only comes when there is nothing between you and the sky for fifty miles.

![Cayden at Fish Springs National Wildlife Refuge](/images/blog/fish-springs-wide.jpg)

I stood there for a long time looking at the landscape. The rock formations in the distance. The layers visible in the cliffs, each one a different color, each one compressed over time into something solid enough to stand on.

And I started thinking about how all of that got there.

Not through one dramatic event. Not through a single force that arrived, did something spectacular, and left. Through layer after layer, placed carefully over time by wind and water and pressure and heat and cold. Each layer thin on its own. Worthless in isolation. But stacked together, compressed together, shaped by everything that came before and after, they became something that has been standing for longer than any of us can comprehend.

That is what mentorship actually is.

---

## The word we got from Homer

The word mentor comes from Homer's Odyssey. Mentor is the trusted friend Odysseus leaves to care for his son Telemachus while he goes off to war. He does not replace the father. He does not become the king. He is a guardian who walks alongside until the young man can walk on his own.

That definition has always captured something that our modern understanding of the word usually misses.

A mentor is not the source of your success. They are not the reason you win. They do not do the work for you or pull you onto the podium with them.

They are the lighthouse. You are still the captain. But without the lighthouse, a lot of captains never find the harbor.

What struck me standing at Fish Springs was that the landscape did not ask for what shaped it. The wind did not ask permission to carve the rock. The water did not check whether the layer was ready to receive it. The forces that built that desert just kept showing up, kept depositing, kept shaping. And over time the accumulated weight of all of it became something extraordinary.

The best mentors in my life have worked the same way. They were not always dramatic. They did not always know they were mentoring me. They were just showing up, depositing something, and trusting that the layers would stack.

---

## A Creator behind every layer

Here is what I kept coming back to out there in the desert.

Those layers did not form randomly. There is a Creator behind the design of that landscape. The forces that shaped it were not accidents. Wind does not randomly carve something beautiful. Water does not randomly deposit layers in a pattern that produces something solid. Behind all of it is intelligence and purpose and a design that was working toward something before the first grain of sediment was ever placed.

The same is true of the people who have shaped you.

I believe that God is involved in who comes into our lives and when. Not controlling, not removing agency, but orchestrating. The mentor who showed up at exactly the right moment, the story someone told you that lit something up inside you before you fully understood why, the person who treated you like who you were becoming before you had any evidence of becoming it. Those are not accidents.

They are layers being placed by a Creator who knows what He is building.

![Fish Springs, Utah](/images/blog/fish-springs-close.jpg)

Winston Churchill once said that to every person there comes in their lifetime that special moment when you are figuratively tapped on the shoulder and offered the chance to do a very special thing, unique to you and your talents. What a tragedy if that moment finds you unprepared or unqualified for the work that could have been your finest hour.

The preparation for that moment is not built in the moment. It is built in the layers that came before. In the people who deposited something in you when you were not even aware you were being shaped. In the stories you collected. In the quiet guidance you received and either took seriously or let fall away.

---

## The layer you are placing right now

I am 22. I am not old enough to be anyone's primary mentor. But I am not too young to be placing layers in someone.

Every person watching how I handle pressure at work is collecting sediment. Every person reading what I write here is being shaped in some small way by what I deposit. Every conversation I have with someone younger and asking honest questions is either placing something worth keeping or something that will have to be removed later.

You are doing the same thing. Whether you know it or not, whether you intend it or not, you are placing layers in people right now. The question is not whether you are doing it. The question is whether what you are depositing is worth compressing into something solid.

Mentorship works like sedimentary rock. Each layer is placed carefully, often quietly, often without the full knowledge of what it is contributing to. But over time the layers compound into something solid enough to stand on, a mountain that could not have been formed by any single deposit alone.

Your balcony moment is coming. The moment you were prepared for by layers you did not choose, placed by people who will never see what they built.

Make sure you are paying attention to what is being placed in you. And make sure what you are placing in others is worth standing on.

The desert at Fish Springs has been building for a long time. Nobody was watching. Nobody was applauding. The layers just kept coming.

That is how it works.
