I was sitting at my kitchen table preparing a Sunday School lesson on the tabernacle.

My notes were already filling the pages. Hebrew word studies. The four colors of the gate. The order of the initiatory in Exodus 40. The two goats of Yom Kippur. I had been at it for hours and I still felt like I was scratching the surface.

Then I pulled up the manual.

The manual is good. It is faithful, careful, and centers Christ. But the manual was not what was coming out of me. The manual would never frame Leviticus 16 the way I was about to frame it on Sunday morning. Not because the doctrine was different. The doctrine was identical. But the way I was going to teach it carried Honduras in it. It carried the swimming pool in it. It carried my mom reading scriptures with me at the kitchen table before I rushed off to a busy day at school. It carried every book I have ever read on leadership, every coaching call I have ever taken, every mentor who ever planted a layer of sediment in me before I knew I was being built.

The doctrine on the page had not moved an inch. The voice carrying it sounded entirely like me.

That is when something clicked that I have not been able to stop thinking about.

There are three things at work in every faithful teacher and every faithful person. Most of us collapse them into one and then get confused when our walk doesn't match our talk or our talk doesn't sound like someone else's talk.

I want you to see them clearly.

**Doctrine. Influence. Expression.**

Doctrine is what cannot move. Influence is the world that shaped you. Expression is what comes out of your mouth and your life when doctrine passes through influence on its way to someone else.

Now stay with me. This is going somewhere bigger than a definition.

In April of 1972, in a priesthood session of General Conference, Paul H. Dunn stood at the pulpit and said he could give an entire sermon in six words. Socrates said know thyself. Cicero said control thyself. The Savior said give thyself. He told the young men in that room to write it down. I wrote it down decades later and built an entire chapter of my book around it.

What I am realizing now is that those six words are the same three words I just gave you.

Know thyself is the doctrine. Control thyself is the influence. Give thyself is the expression.

---

## Know Thyself — Doctrine

Doctrine is the fixed star.

The Father has a body. Christ atoned for you personally. The covenant path leads home. Priesthood keys are real. Intelligence is light and truth. These do not bend because you are tired. They do not bend because the culture moved. They do not bend because someone with a microphone said something contradictory on the internet last week. They are fixed. And the first work of any leader, any teacher, any disciple, is to know what cannot move.

Know thyself does not start with personality tests. It starts with knowing the doctrine that defines what you are. You are not a brand. You are not a job title. You are not the sum of your accomplishments or your followers or your network. You are a son or daughter of God with a divine inheritance and an eternal trajectory. That is the doctrine. That is the fixed star.

Until you know it at the level of conviction, every other voice in your life will be louder than the one telling you the truth.

I learned this at thirteen years old in a quiet room at the end of a hall. I did not just receive a testimony of a book in that moment. I found out who I was. The room was completely silent. And then something moved through me that I have never been able to fully describe to another person. A peace I had never known before. And it has held every single time the world tried to tell me a different version of who I am.

That is the doctrine. That is what cannot move.

You cannot control what you do not know. So know it first.

---

## Control Thyself — Influence

Now influence.

This is the part most people get wrong. They either treat their influences as enemies of the doctrine, things to be erased and ashamed of, or they treat them as authorities equal to the doctrine, which is how good people drift into bad places one cultural concession at a time.

Influence is neither.

Influence is everything that shaped how you receive, process, and carry the doctrine. Your family. Your geography. Your profession. Your language. The decade you were born in. The mentors who poured into you. The losses that scarred you. The wins that calibrated you. The books you read at fifteen that you forgot you read but still think with. The coaches who pulled you aside and said one sentence that changed everything.

None of this is the doctrine. All of it shapes how you receive it.

President Joseph F. Smith and President Spencer W. Kimball both taught that God has given portions of His light to philosophers, poets, religious leaders, and reformers across every culture and century. Not because those people had the priesthood. Because truth circumscribes into one great whole, and every honest seeker stumbles onto pieces of it. Plato glimpsed the body and spirit as different in kind. Frankl found in the camps that meaning is the power source of survival. Maxwell built a whole career on the laws of how good people lead. Every one of them was reaching, and every one of them got pieces. The Restoration is the recovery of the whole. But the pieces still belong to the whole.

Control thyself is the work of choosing which influences you let into the house and which you keep on the porch. It is also the work of honoring the good in every influence God allowed to shape you.

I served my mission in Honduras. I watched people get baptized who came from Catholic homes, Evangelical homes, atheist homes, indigenous traditions older than any of those. Not one of them arrived at the font as a blank slate. They arrived carrying their whole lives. And the Spirit did not erase what they brought. The Spirit consecrated it.

This is what control thyself actually means. Not erasure. Not denial. Not pretending you have no influences. Stewardship. You take an honest inventory of what shaped you. You let the doctrine refine the pieces that need refining. You honor the pieces that were already light. And you stop being ashamed that you sound like someone with a story.

You have a story. The Lord chose your story before you were born. He did not make a mistake when He gave you yours.

Control the influence. Don't erase it.

---

## Give Thyself — Expression

And then comes expression.

Expression is what happens when doctrine passes through your particular life on its way to someone else's. It is the sermon you preach. The product you build. The blog post you write. The coaching call you take. The way you father your children. The way you lead a meeting. The way you talk about Christ with the people who ride in your car.

Expression is not doctrine. Two faithful teachers can teach the exact same doctrine and sound entirely different because their expression carries their influence. That is not a problem. That is the design.

Think about a stained glass window in a chapel. The light coming through the window is one light. It is the same sun reaching every pane. But the colors of the panes are different, and so the room is illuminated by the integration of all of them together. Take any one pane out and the room is dimmer. Try to make every pane the same color and you have killed the design.

Your influence is the colored glass. The doctrine is the light. The room you walk into needs the light to come through your particular pane, or the room loses something only your color could have given it.

Now hear this carefully. The light does not change because of the glass. The doctrine is unchanged. But the room is different because the light came through you and not someone else.

That is expression. That is give thyself.

---

## The Progression Matters

In Chapter 7 of Under Construction, I wrote that these three are not three separate ideas. They are one progression. You cannot control what you do not know. You cannot give what you have not controlled. The sequence is the doctrine of becoming.

A leader who tries to give without first knowing will give a version of themselves that has no fixed star. They will adapt to the room. They will say what the audience wants to hear. They will become whatever the moment rewards. That is not generosity. That is performance.

A leader who tries to give without first controlling will pour their unchecked influences into the room and call it authenticity. The bull who never learned breath control will run people over and call it passion. The person who never reckoned with their formative wounds will project them onto every team they ever lead. That is not service. That is leakage.

But a leader who knows the doctrine, controls the influence, and then gives themselves through their refined expression is doing something the world genuinely needs. They are letting the unchanging light pass through their particular pane and illuminating a room nobody else could illuminate the same way.

That is the kind of leader worth following. That is the kind of teacher worth listening to. That is the kind of person worth becoming.

---

## Why This Matters Right Now

I am writing this from the middle of building. I am still a Product Manager at Caselle. I am still building Govsight. I am still teaching Sunday School every other Sunday. I do not know how any of it ends.

But I know what cannot change. I know the doctrine.

I know what shaped me. I know my influences and I am trying every day to control them with honesty rather than denial. The swimming. The mission. The accounting. The mentors. The losses. The wins. The God who has been in my corner since I was thirteen years old in a quiet room at the end of a hall.

And I am trying to give what comes out of that integration to whoever is in the room. The blog reader. The Sunday School class. The product team. The city clerk who will never know my name but will use the software my team built. Every word I write or speak is meant to be a testament of the Savior, and the way it comes out is going to sound like Cayden because Cayden is the pane the light is passing through.

That is not a limitation. That is the design.

I want you to do the same.

Anchor in the doctrine that does not move. Take an honest inventory of the influences that shaped you and steward them with grace. And then give the world what only you can give, because nobody else has your particular pane and the room is darker without your color in it.

The doctrine is the light. Your influence is the glass. Your expression is the room when the two meet.

Know thyself. Control thyself. Give thyself.

The house was never for you. It was always for the people who would live in it.

Now go light the room.
