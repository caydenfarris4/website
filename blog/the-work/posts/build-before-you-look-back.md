I showed up to my first swim practice in regular swim trunks.

Not a technical suit. Not the gear that signals to everyone in the pool that you belong there. Just a pair of normal swim trunks because that was all I owned. I looked around and saw people who had been competing for years — the equipment, the times, the quiet confidence of people who knew exactly what they were doing. And then there was me.

Within the first hour I had thrown up twice from exhaustion.

After that practice, the coach called everyone together for goal-setting. I had no idea what a good time looked like. I had no framework for what was even possible. But I set three goals anyway — Varsity sophomore year, qualify for State junior year, win State senior year.

I told my coach those goals about an hour after meeting her. She said they were extremely lofty. That most people who win state have been swimming for years and I had a lot of ground to make up. She wasn't trying to lower my standard. She was just being honest about what accepting those goals actually meant.

What I didn't understand yet was that the house I was building had just started. And I had no idea how long construction actually takes.

## Most leadership books are written from the penthouse

The author has already built the company, won the championship, turned the team around. They hand you the wisdom from the top floor and tell you to start climbing. The hindsight is clean. The lessons are tidy. The ending is already written.

I am not writing from the penthouse. I am twenty-two years old and I am on the job site right now. I do not know how Govsight ends. I do not know how the product owner role I stepped into on April 6th turns out. I am in the middle of figuring out the same things you are figuring out.

John Maxwell said it as clearly as anyone — success is knowing your purpose in life, growing to reach your maximum potential, and sowing seeds that benefit others. Notice what is missing from that definition. A finish line. Because there isn't one. There is only the building.

The most important construction project of your life is the person you are becoming. And the best time to build it intentionally is before you look back and discover you built something you didn't mean to.

---

## The house gets built either way

Here is the thing about construction. The house doesn't wait for you to decide you're ready to build it. It goes up anyway. Every day. Every decision. Every habit you started without thinking about where it leads.

The house is going up. The question is whether you're building it or it's building itself.

> "Hindsight is a gift given too late to be fully useful. Foresight — asking who you're becoming before the answer reveals itself — is rarer and worth more."

My junior year I had given everything I knew how to give. Early mornings, extra workouts, a commitment I had never applied to anything else. And then the season ended and I looked at my times and the clock had barely moved. I was the alternate at State — the 25th ranked swimmer in the state, one spot away from actually racing.

I sat next to that pool for two days watching races I wasn't in.

That feeling became fuel. Not because I decided it would. Because something had been building in me that I hadn't fully seen yet — a foundation I was laying without knowing what I was laying it for. A month later the pandemic hit and practices were canceled. I had to work out alone. No teammates. No coach watching. Accountable only to the goals I had written on goal day as a sophomore in my regular swim trunks.

That is where the foundation is actually built. Not in the moments anyone is watching. In the ones they aren't.

## The frame before the house looks like anything

Senior year I made a decision. I joined a club team coached by a former college breaststroker. He put me in the water with faster swimmers immediately — not to humiliate me, but to give my effort somewhere to aim. Then he told me to go watch Adam Peaty.

Study how elite breaststrokers move. See the controlled grace. He called me a bull. He meant it as a diagnosis. I had power but no control. I was crashing through the water instead of moving through it.

He had me slow my stroke all the way down. Own each one completely before adding speed. It felt like backwards. But he kept reminding me — breaststroke is about grace and power, not the number of strokes. Slow down to build the system, and the speed follows.

A month later I dropped from a 1:05 to a 1:02.

Abraham Lincoln said give me six hours to chop down a tree and I'll spend the first four sharpening the axe. Most people read that as a lesson about patience. I read it as a lesson about framing. You don't earn the right to swing hard by wanting it badly enough. You earn it by building the system that makes every swing count.

I started my product owner role at Caselle on April 6th, 2026. But I had been building the frame for weeks before that day. I wrote a 90-day strategy before I sat at my desk for the first time. I mapped integration architecture. I built ROI calculators for features that didn't exist yet. None of that was assigned to me. No one asked for it.

I did it because two swimming coaches had already taught me that effort without a frame is just reaction.

The people who consistently raise the ceiling in every room they enter didn't learn that in the room. They built it long before they arrived.

## You're already building

We got to State my senior year. I was a top 10 qualifier. I dove in and the first 50 yards felt right. Then my throat closed. Everything went dark. My arms went numb. I touched the wall and couldn't pull myself out of the pool. My coach helped me out. I couldn't feel my body or open my eyes for hours.

I had failed the individual race. Three years of work gone in 60 seconds of an asthma attack I couldn't control.

The next morning my relay team needed me. 20 seconds. That was all. We were in the A-final and had a real shot. I got back on the blocks, said a prayer, and swam the only race I had left in me that day.

We won.

Standing on that podium with my brothers, I realized something that changed how I understood all of it. I had spent three years imagining myself up there as an individual champion. What I actually felt was bigger than anything that version of the story could have produced.

M. Russell Ballard said it this way — if we don't set goals in our life and learn to master the techniques of living to reach them, we can reach a ripe old age and look back only to see we reached but a small part of our full potential.

I am twenty-two. I don't know how my story ends. But I know this: whatever is being built in the quiet seasons right now will be there when the inspection comes. The work you do when nobody is watching is the only work that holds under real pressure.

So the question isn't whether you're building. You are. The question is whether you're building it on purpose.
