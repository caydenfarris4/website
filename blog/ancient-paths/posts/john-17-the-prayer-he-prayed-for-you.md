Before we talk about what this prayer means, I want to put you in the room.

It is the night before Gethsemane. The Passover meal is finished. Judas has already left to do what he is going to do. Jesus has washed the disciples' feet, given the bread and the cup, and told them things they do not fully understand yet. He knows, with complete clarity, that within hours He will be arrested. Within hours after that, He will be dead.

What would you do with that time?

Most of us, I think, would spend it on ourselves. Getting affairs in order. Saying goodbye to the people we love. Trying to absorb the reality of what is coming. The weight of knowing exactly how the next 24 hours unfold would be almost impossible to carry without collapsing under it.

Jesus spends those hours praying. Not for Himself, not primarily. For His disciples. And for everyone who would ever believe because of them.

That means He was praying for you. That night. In that room. With Gethsemane waiting on the other side of the prayer.

That is the context. Now let's look at what He actually said.

---

## Verse 3 — the most important definition in scripture

> "And this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent."
> — John 17:3

Most people read that verse and move on. But the word translated "know" in this verse is doing more work than the English lets you see.

In Spanish, there are two words for the verb to know. Saber means to know information, to know facts about something. Conocer means to know relationally, to know personally, the way you know a friend versus the way you know a stranger.

The Greek underneath this verse uses the relational word. Not knowing about God. Knowing God.

Eternal life is not a reward for having correct theological information. It is the ongoing experience of a genuine relationship with the Divine. Christ is not describing a destination you arrive at after death. He is describing a quality of knowing that begins now and extends forever.

This matters enormously for how you approach scripture, prayer, and the covenants you have made. The question is not only what do you know about God. The question is do you know Him. Is there a relationship there, or just information?

Information can be doubted and debated and eventually dismantled. Relationship holds when everything else shakes.

---

## The prayer itself — what He is actually asking for

John 17 moves through three movements. Jesus prays for Himself, then for His disciples, then for everyone who will believe through their words. Each movement builds on the last.

When He prays for Himself, He asks the Father to glorify Him so that He can glorify the Father. The purpose of His glorification is not personal. It is so that eternal life — that knowing relationship — can be given to everyone the Father has given Him.

When He prays for His disciples, He asks not that they would be taken out of the world but that they would be kept from evil while they remain in it. He asks that they would be sanctified through truth. He says something remarkable in verse 19.

> "And for their sakes I sanctify myself, that they also might be sanctified through the truth."
> — John 17:19

He sets Himself apart — consecrates Himself entirely — so that they can be set apart. His consecration makes their sanctification possible. What He is about to do in Gethsemane and on the cross is not just substitutionary. It is generative. His giving produces their becoming.

---

## Verse 20 — the verse that stops everything

Then He says this.

> "Neither pray I for these alone, but for them also which shall believe on me through their word."
> — John 17:20

He is not only praying for the eleven men in the room. He is praying for everyone who will ever believe because of what those eleven men say and write and pass on. Every person who has ever read John, or Matthew, or Romans, or any of the New Testament. Every person who has heard the gospel through a missionary or a parent or a friend.

He is praying for you. Specifically. On that night. In that room. With Gethsemane waiting.

I do not know what to do with that except sit with it for a long time. He knew you were coming. He knew the chain of people and words and moments that would eventually reach you. And He prayed for you by name in the hours before His most important work.

---

## What He is asking for you

> "That they all may be one; as thou, Father, art in me, and I in thee, that they also may be one in us: that the world may believe that thou hast sent me."
> — John 17:21

The Greek word behind "as" in that verse is kathos. It does not mean approximately. It does not mean similar to or kind of like. It means exactly as, in the same way, to the same degree.

He is not asking that we would have a nice sense of community. He is asking that the unity we experience with each other and with God would be the same kind of unity that exists between the Father and the Son.

That is a staggering standard. And He prays for it as though it is actually possible. Not because He is being naive, but because He is about to make it possible. The atonement He is hours away from performing is precisely what makes that level of unity accessible to fallen people.

Then He tells you why it matters.

> "That the world may know that thou hast sent me."
> — John 17:23

The proof of the gospel to the watching world is not your argument. It is your unity. The degree to which you are genuinely one with God and one with each other is the degree to which the world around you sees evidence that Christ was sent.

That is a missionary statement buried inside a prayer. The way you live, the unity of your covenants, the degree to which distractions and divisions have been resolved into a single focus on God, these things are not just personal spiritual matters. They are public witness.

---

## The closing — where He wants to take you

The prayer ends with something that takes a moment to absorb.

> "Father, I will that they also, whom thou hast given me, be with me where I am; that they may behold my glory."
> — John 17:24

He does not ask that you would make it to a good place after this life. He asks that you would be with Him where He is. Not near Him. With Him. The same preposition used for the relationship between Father and Son.

And then the last verse.

> "I in them, and thou in me, that they may be made perfect in one."
> — John 17:23

The Greek word translated perfect here is teleios. It does not mean without flaw. It means complete, finished, arrived at the intended end. It is a covenant completion word. The thing has become what it was always meant to become.

That is what He is praying for you. Not perfection in the sense of flawlessness. Completion. The full arrival of what God always intended when He made you.

He prayed that prayer in a room in Jerusalem, hours before Gethsemane, knowing exactly what was coming, knowing your name, knowing the chain of words and people that would reach you.

The prayer is still being answered.

You are part of the answer.
