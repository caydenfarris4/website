I want to start with a question before we get into the word itself.

What is the last word of the Old Testament?

Not the last book. Not the last chapter. The last word - the final thing God says before 400 years of silence fall over Israel and the entire Hebrew canon closes.

Most people have never thought about this. We read scripture in sections, in lessons, in chunks. We don't think about the Old Testament as a complete literary unit with a deliberate ending. But it is. And the ending is not a comfort. It is not a promise of peace. It is not a benediction.

It is a threat.

The Hebrew Bible ends on the word **cherem**.

---

## What cherem actually means

This is one of those Hebrew words that carries more than one meaning - and both meanings matter for what we are going to trace.

**חֵרֶם - cherem** carries two connected ideas:

The first is **sacred consecration** - something so completely devoted to God that it can never be reclaimed for ordinary use. In this sense cherem is total dedication. The object, the person, the thing belongs entirely to God and cannot be taken back.

The second - and this is where it gets heavy - is **sacred destruction**. Something under cherem must be completely destroyed. Not plundered. Not partially spared. Annihilated. Because it belongs to God and cannot be touched by human hands for human purposes.

Both meanings are present simultaneously. The destruction IS the consecration. The thing is given entirely to God by being removed entirely from human use. Think of it as the dark side of the same coin that gives us the word holy - set apart, untouchable, belonging to another realm.

Now watch where this word shows up in Israel's history.

---

## Jericho - cherem as command

When Israel enters the promised land and approaches Jericho, God gives a specific command.

> "And the city shall be accursed [cherem], even it, and all that are therein, to the Lord."
> - Joshua 6:17

Everything in Jericho - the gold, the silver, the livestock, the people - is under cherem. It belongs to God. None of it can be touched. None of it can be kept. It is devoted entirely to destruction as an act of consecration.

The walls fall. The city burns. Israel obeys.

Except one man doesn't.

Achan sees a Babylonian garment and some silver and gold. He takes them. He hides them under his tent. A private decision, made when nobody was watching, in the chaos of a burning city.

The next battle - a small city called Ai - Israel loses. Thirty-six men die. Joshua falls on his face before God and asks why. And God's answer stops everything.

> "There is an accursed thing [cherem] in the midst of thee, O Israel: thou canst not stand before thine enemies, until ye take away the accursed thing from among you."
> - Joshua 7:13

One man's private violation of cherem made the entire nation unable to stand. That is how serious this is. Cherem is not personal. It is communal. What one person touches that belongs to God affects everyone around them.

Achan is found. He confesses. He is stoned, and everything he took is burned.

The cherem is resolved. Israel fights again. This time they win.

## Saul and Agag - the cost of partial obedience

Several hundred years later, God gives King Saul a command through Samuel the prophet.

Go destroy the Amalekites. Everything. Completely. Cherem.

> "Now go and smite Amalek, and utterly destroy [cherem] all that they have, and spare them not."
> - 1 Samuel 15:3

Saul goes. He fights. He wins. And then he makes a decision that will echo for five hundred years.

He keeps the best livestock. His reason sounds reasonable - he was going to sacrifice them to God. And he keeps Agag, the king of Amalek, alive.

Partial obedience. Good intentions. And Samuel's response is one of the most direct confrontations in all of scripture.

> "Hath the Lord as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the Lord? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice, and to hearken than the fat of rams."
> - 1 Samuel 15:22

Saul wanted to give God something good. God had asked for something specific. Those are not the same thing.

Samuel hews Agag to pieces himself. But the damage is done. The cherem was not completed. And here is what most people never connect.

Five hundred years later, a man named Haman rises to power in the Persian court and nearly destroys every Jew in the known world. His genealogy is recorded in the book of Esther: he is an **Agagite**. A descendant of the king Saul was commanded to destroy and didn't.

Saul's incomplete obedience to cherem produced a threat to Israel five centuries later. The thing he spared came back. Haman is the bill coming due on a debt Saul refused to pay.

It is Mordecai - from the tribe of Benjamin, the same tribe as Saul - who finally finishes what Saul started. And Esther, at risk of her own life, intercedes to reverse the decree of death.

But I want you to hold that thread - **incomplete obedience to cherem has generational consequences** - because we are going to need it at the end.

## The last word of the Old Testament

Now we arrive at Malachi. The last prophet. The last book. The last chapter. The last verse.

God is speaking through Malachi about the coming of Elijah before the great and dreadful day of the Lord. He says Elijah will come and turn the hearts of fathers to their children and children to their fathers.

And then the Hebrew canon closes with this:

> "Lest I come and smite the earth with a curse."
> - Malachi 4:6

That word translated "curse" in your English Bible?

**Cherem.**

The entire Hebrew Bible - Genesis through Malachi, the law and the prophets, the covenants and the history and the poetry and the warnings - ends on a conditional threat of sacred destruction hanging over the earth.

Not a promise. Not a comfort. A threat with a condition attached.

The condition is Elijah coming. The hearts of fathers turning to children. The hearts of children turning to fathers. If that doesn't happen - cherem. The earth under sacred destruction.

And then silence. Four hundred years of it.

## What Moroni said to Joseph Smith

Now I want you to think about the context of September 1823. Joseph Smith is seventeen years old. He has had one vision three years earlier that nobody believes. He is a farm boy in New York with no theological training, no institutional support, and no idea what is coming.

An angel appears in his room in the middle of the night.

The first thing Moroni quotes to him - the opening scripture of the entire Restoration message - is Malachi 4. The cherem verse. The last word of the Old Testament.

Why does God open the Restoration by pointing to the threat that closed the Old Testament?

Because the Restoration is the answer to that threat.

The 400 years of silence between Malachi and the New Testament were not God abandoning Israel. They were the gap between the threat and its resolution. The priesthood had been lost. The sealing authority - the power to bind families across generations - had been taken from the earth. Without it, the hearts of fathers cannot be truly turned to children because there is nothing that can seal that bond beyond death.

The cherem threat hangs over the earth precisely because the sealing keys are gone.

## D&C 110 - the loop closes

April 3, 1836. The Kirtland Temple has just been dedicated. Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery retire behind the veil for prayer.

The Lord appears. Moses appears and commits the keys of the gathering of Israel. Elias appears and commits the dispensation of the gospel of Abraham.

And then:

> "After this vision had closed, another great and glorious vision burst upon us; for Elijah the prophet, who was taken to heaven without tasting death, stood before us, and said: Behold, the time has fully come, which was spoken of by the mouth of Malachi - testifying that he [Elijah] should be sent, before the great and dreadful day of the Lord come - To turn the hearts of the fathers to the children, and the children to the fathers, lest the whole earth be smitten with a curse."
> - D&C 110:13-15

Elijah quotes the cherem verse directly. He knows exactly what he is there to resolve.

And then he commits the sealing keys.

The last word of the Old Testament - the unresolved cherem threat hanging over the earth since Malachi - is answered in a room in Kirtland, Ohio, in 1836. The sealing power returns to the earth. The hearts of fathers can now be turned to children not just emotionally but covenantally, sealed by priesthood authority that reaches across death.

The threat of cherem is resolved by the very thing cherem always pointed to - total consecration to God. Not the consecration of destruction, but the consecration of families sealed to Him across generations.

## What this means in the temple today

Every proxy ordinance performed in a temple is an act of anti-cherem.

The sealing keys Elijah restored make it possible for the work of salvation to reach people who died without it. Fathers sealed to children across centuries. Families gathered not just in mortality but eternally.

Joseph Smith taught that we cannot be made perfect without our dead, and they cannot be made perfect without us. Hebrews 11:40 says the same thing - the ancient faithful "without us should not be made perfect."

Saul's incomplete obedience to cherem had consequences for five hundred years. Our incomplete obedience to the sealing work has consequences of the same kind - people waiting on the other side of the veil for someone in mortality to do the work.

The Hebrew Bible ends on a threat. The Restoration resolves it. And every name extracted from a genealogical record, every ordinance performed by proxy, every heart turned toward fathers and children is a stone placed in the answer to the last word of the Old Testament.

The cherem is being resolved. One family at a time.
