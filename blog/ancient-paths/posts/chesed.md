Hannah is in the temple at Shiloh, praying so quietly that her lips move without sound.

The high priest of Israel is watching her from across the court and he has reached the wrong conclusion. He thinks she is drunk. He walks over to rebuke her. "How long wilt thou be drunken? put away thy wine from thee" (1 Samuel 1:14).

She is not drunk. She is praying for a son. She has prayed for years. The temple of Israel cannot tell the difference between a drunk woman and a woman carrying the future of the nation in her silent grief. The most spiritually attentive man in Israel misreads what is happening directly in front of him.

But God does not.

A year later Hannah has Samuel. Samuel becomes the prophet who anoints both Saul and David. The Davidic line, which leads to Christ, exists because a woman prayed in a way her own high priest could not understand.

This is the pattern.

The covenant moves through women. It always has. And it moves most powerfully through the kind of women whose work is quiet enough that the world misses it.

I want to give you a Hebrew word to carry through this entire post.

**Chesed** (חֶסֶד).

English translators have rendered chesed as lovingkindness, mercy, steadfast love, covenant loyalty, faithfulness. None of those captures it fully. Chesed is the kind of love that shows up because of covenant relationship. It does more than is required. It endures when the other party does not deserve it. It quietly holds what would otherwise fall apart, and it does not ask for credit for the holding.

Chesed is what God does for His people when they have forgotten Him.

Chesed is what a mother does when no one is watching.

The two are not unrelated. The Hebrew Bible uses chesed to describe both, and the parallel is not an accident.

---

## Ruth

Ruth is the most concentrated portrait of chesed in scripture. The book uses the word three times to describe her, which by Hebrew literary convention means the entire story is built around it.

She is a Moabite. An outsider. Her husband is dead. Her father-in-law is dead. Her sister-in-law has gone home to her own people, which is the wise and culturally expected thing to do. There is no advantage in staying with Naomi. Ruth has nothing to gain and everything to lose.

She binds herself to Naomi anyway.

> "Whither thou goest, I will go; and where thou lodgest, I will lodge: thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God."
> — Ruth 1:16

It is one of the most beautiful covenant statements in the Old Testament, and it is spoken by a foreign widow to a bereaved old woman who can offer her nothing in return.

Boaz notices her almost immediately. He says of her, "thou hast shewed more kindness in the latter end than at the beginning" (Ruth 3:10). The Hebrew word translated kindness is chesed. He recognizes that Ruth's chesed toward Naomi mirrors God's chesed toward Israel. He marries her. They have a son named Obed. Obed has a son named Jesse. Jesse has a son named David.

The book of Ruth ends with that genealogy. Read it carefully.

Christ comes through a Gentile widow who chose the covenant when nothing required her to.

Matthew names her in the genealogy of Jesus. He only names four women in that lineage, and Ruth is one of them. The Lord deliberately wrote a chesed-keeping woman into the bloodline of His Son.

## Elisabeth

Six months before Christ is born, an old woman feels her unborn son leap inside her.

Mary has just walked into Elisabeth's house. She has not announced anything. She has not told Elisabeth what the angel said. She has not explained why a young unmarried woman has traveled into the hill country alone.

Elisabeth knows anyway.

The Spirit speaks to her before Mary opens her mouth. Elisabeth recognizes the Lord in Mary's womb before any prophet, any priest, any apostle, any Pharisee in Israel. The first prophecy of Christ's identity in the New Testament comes out of an old woman's mouth in a back-country village.

"Whence is this to me, that the mother of my Lord should come to me?" (Luke 1:43).

Elisabeth identifies Christ as Lord. She names Him before John can speak, before Mary has given birth, before any of the men of the temple have any idea what is unfolding.

Two women meet in a kitchen and the New Testament begins.

---

## Mary

The Word becomes flesh through her body.

Her response to the angel is one sentence. "Be it unto me according to thy word" (Luke 1:38). It is the most consequential yes in the history of mortality, and it is given by a teenager in Nazareth who has every earthly reason to refuse.

Imagine her position. An unmarried girl in first-century Galilee being told she is going to be pregnant by the Holy Ghost and asked if she will accept it. The cultural penalty for that pregnancy is stoning. The social penalty is permanent disgrace. Joseph could have walked away. Her family could have rejected her. The rabbis could have condemned her.

She said yes anyway.

She carries the Creator. She nurses the Atoner. She raises the Savior. She stands at the cross when most of the apostles have scattered. She is in the upper room at Pentecost. From the Annunciation to the founding of the Church, Mary is present for every hinge moment of the Restoration of all things.

She is a chosen, prepared, faithful woman through whom the Atonement entered mortality. The Atonement required her chesed. Her single sentence held the entire plan together at the moment it could have collapsed.

That is chesed. That is covenant loyalty doing what only it can do.

## Mary Magdalene

It is still dark on the third day when she walks to the tomb.

The men have scattered. Peter has denied. Most of the disciples have hidden. The Sabbath has passed and the women have come back to do the burial work the men cannot bring themselves to do.

Mary Magdalene arrives first.

The tomb is empty. She runs to find Peter and John. They come, look, and leave. The text is striking on this point. The men go back to where they were. Mary stays. She stands outside the tomb weeping because the work is not finished and she cannot leave.

A man she takes for the gardener asks her why she is crying. She does not recognize Him.

He says her name. Mary.

She turns and the Resurrection has a witness.

The risen Christ commissions her with a message that no apostle has yet carried. "Go to my brethren, and say unto them, I ascend unto my Father, and your Father; and to my God, and your God" (John 20:17). The most consequential news in human history, the Resurrection itself, is first carried by a woman to a group of men who will at first refuse to believe her.

The early Church called her the apostle to the apostles.

She earned the title because she stayed.

## My Mother

I want to tell you something about my own mother.

For two years on my mission in Honduras, I received an email from her every single Sunday without fail. Not one Sunday passed without it. Sometimes the subject line was just a date. When I saw a date, I knew the contents were going to undo me. Every Monday when I called home, she was on the Google Meet within fifteen seconds of the ring. There was never a delay. She was two thousand miles away and she answered like she was in the next room.

While I was raising my hands to teach in Honduras, she was raising hers in prayer in Pleasant Grove. While I was studying the gospel in Spanish, she was studying it at our kitchen table before the rest of us came down for school. She has taught Come Follow Me every week since the new manual came out in 2019. I remember her taking my dad to the temple when I wasn't even old enough to grasp where they were going. The decisions in my life that I am most grateful for were almost all influenced by her in ways I did not see at the time and only see now.

She made a promise to my sister, who was not sure she wanted to go to college, that they would go together. So my mother went back to school while raising three children still at home. She had never finished her degree. She did it because she had told her daughter she would, and chesed does not break promises just because they are inconvenient.

She is the rock of our family. She is the quiet influence behind every good decision I have ever made. She is the chesed that has carried our home for as long as I can remember and longer than I have understood.

You probably have a woman like that in your life. A mother. A grandmother. An aunt. A sister. A wife. A teacher. A friend. Someone whose covenant loyalty has held the structure of your life together so consistently that you have stopped noticing it because the work is so reliable it has become invisible.

This is your invitation to notice.

## Before Mother's Day

Hannah's chesed produced a prophet who anointed kings.

Ruth's chesed produced the genealogy of Christ.

Elisabeth's chesed produced the first prophecy of Christ's Lordship in the New Testament.

Mary's chesed produced the Atonement.

Mary Magdalene's chesed produced the first witness of the Resurrection.

The Restoration was scribed by Emma. The Smith family was held together by Lucy. Your covenant lineage in this dispensation rests on women whose names most members do not say nearly often enough.

And somewhere in that lineage of chesed sits your mother. The woman who fed you before the day swallowed you. The woman who prayed for you when you did not know to pray for yourself. The woman whose intuition pointed you toward the right turn at the right moment more times than you have counted. The woman whose covenant loyalty has held your life together in ways that may not become fully visible to you until you have children of your own.

Mother's Day is coming.

Do not let it pass with a card and a phone call. Sit down and write her the letter that names what she has actually done. Tell her you see the chesed. Tell her you understand now what you did not understand at fifteen. The meals and the prayers and the late-night conversations and the quiet pushes toward the right decisions were the covenant moving through her hands into your life.

Tell her thank you in language specific enough that she knows you mean it.

The covenant moves through women. It always has. And the woman through whom the covenant first moved into you deserves to know that you finally see her.

She earned it the way Ruth earned it, the way Hannah earned it, the way Mary earned it.

Quietly. Faithfully. Year after year. Without ever asking for the credit.

Now give it to her.
