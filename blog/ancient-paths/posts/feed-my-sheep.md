Picture this. The charcoal fire is still warm on the beach.

Three days ago Peter had stood at another charcoal fire, the only other one John's Gospel mentions, and denied he knew Jesus. Three times. In that moment of betrayal, the rooster crowed. He went out and wept bitterly.

Now he's back to fishing. The thing he knew before all of this. Maybe he felt disqualified. Maybe he couldn't face what he had done. I wonder if he blamed himself for what had happened to his Lord.

Nonetheless, he went back to something that he felt he couldn't mess up on.

Jesus appears on the shore, unrecognized at first. Tells them to cast the net on the other side. 153 fish. John realizes who it is. Peter throws himself into the water. The Messiah has returned and awaits his apostles on the shore.

They eat together on the beach. And then Jesus turns to Peter and asks the question that must have felt like it reached into his chest.

"Simon, son of Jonas, lovest thou me?"

Not Peter. Simon. His old name. Before the calling to the holy apostleship. Jesus is speaking to the man underneath the apostle.

Three times He asks. Once for every denial. Peter is grieved by the third, "Lord, thou knowest all things," which is itself an acknowledgment. You know what I did. You know what I am.

And every single time, regardless of the answer, Jesus says the same thing.

Feed my lambs. Feed my sheep. Feed my sheep.

Jesus doesn't bring up the failure. He meets Peter where he is. He helps him rediscover his love for his Redeemer and immediately points it outward. Toward other people. Toward service. Toward those who need to be fed.

That is what Christ does with our love. He doesn't let it sit. He redirects it.

---

I wasn't at a beach. I was at a sacrament table.

A dear friend leaned over and asked me a simple question. Had I ever thought about becoming a temple worker?

It had kept me up at night. The thought had lived in me quietly for a while. I almost did it while studying up at Utah State, but I hadn't acted on it fully. It was outside my comfort zone. And here was someone, at the moment I was participating in one of the most sacred ordinances we have, asking me to step into something bigger. What may have been a simple question penetrated my soul deeply.

Christ has a way of speaking through people we trust.

The temple is where I understood what Jesus meant when He said feed my sheep. Because in that holy place, you are serving people who cannot serve themselves. People who lived and died without these ordinances. You stand in as a proxy for someone you have never met and will not meet in this life, and you love them anyway.

That is the love the Risen Christ was asking Peter for on that beach.

Not a feeling. An action. Directed outward. Extended to strangers. Sustained by covenant.

Because of Him, I love.
